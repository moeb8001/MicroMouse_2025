Chapter 1

Light & Lasers

A laser produces an intense, perfectly parallel beam of a single color of light. Understanding what that means — and what numbers matter — is the foundation of everything else.

What Is Light?

Light is electromagnetic radiation. It has a wavelength (λ), measured in nanometers (nm). Wavelength determines color: 400 nm = violet, 532 nm = green, 700 nm = red. Below 400 nm is ultraviolet (UV); above 700 nm is infrared (IR) — both invisible.

Light also comes in discrete packets called photons. Each photon carries energy E = hc/λ, where h is Planck's constant and c is the speed of light. A green photon (532 nm) carries 3.7 × 10⁻¹⁹ joules. That's tiny — you need billions of photons to see anything.

What Makes a Laser Special?

A laser differs from a lamp or LED in three ways:

① Monochromatic — all photons have exactly the same wavelength. A green laser at 532 nm means every photon is 532 nm, not "greenish." This matters because optical filters can isolate exactly this wavelength and reject all background light.

② Coherent — all photon waves are in phase (crests aligned). This allows the beam to stay collimated (parallel) over long distances and to form thin, sharp light sheets.

③ Directional — the beam diverges very little. A flashlight spreads; a laser pointer stays a dot across a room. This lets you focus enormous power into a tiny volume.

Your Laser: Nd:YAG at 532 nm

Nd:YAG is a solid-state laser. The crystal naturally produces infrared light at 1064 nm, but a frequency doubling crystal inside the laser converts this to visible green at 532 nm — exactly half the wavelength (double the frequency). Nd:YAG lasers are the workhorse of PIV/PTV because they produce high energy in short pulses.

Sheet thickness determines two things: ① the local light intensity (thinner = brighter per particle, because the same energy is concentrated into a smaller volume) and ② how many particles are illuminated (thinner = fewer particles per frame). There's an optimum — which is what you're exploring.

Fluence: The Number That Matters

The laser energy arrives at the measurement plane as a sheet with area = height × thickness. The fluence (energy per unit area) determines how many photons hit each particle:

Fluence = Epulse / (sheet height × sheet thickness)    [J/m²]
Example: 100 mJ / (20 mm × 1 mm) = 100×10⁻³ / (0.02 × 0.001) = 5,000 J/m²

Higher fluence → more photons per particle → stronger scattered signal → easier to detect small particles. You can increase fluence by: increasing laser energy, narrowing the sheet, or reducing the sheet height (at the cost of smaller FOV).

Chapter 2

Camera Sensors

A camera sensor converts photons into electronic signals. Understanding the sensor types and their specs tells you how faint a signal the camera can see.

How a Sensor Works (30-Second Version)

A camera sensor is a grid of millions of tiny light-sensitive cells called pixels. When a photon hits a pixel, it knocks loose an electron (via the photoelectric effect). The pixel accumulates electrons during the exposure. After exposure, the accumulated charge is read out as a number. More photons → more electrons → higher number → brighter pixel in the image.

Think of each pixel as a rain gauge. During a brief rainstorm (the laser pulse), some gauges catch more rain (photons) than others. After the storm, you read how much water is in each gauge. That's your image.

Sensor Types: CCD vs. CMOS vs. sCMOS

CCD

Charge-Coupled Device. The original digital camera sensor. Electrons are shifted pixel-by-pixel to a single readout amplifier, like a bucket brigade. Very uniform, low noise — but slow to read out because everything goes through one bottleneck. Used in older scientific cameras.

CMOS

Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor. Each pixel has its own amplifier — all pixels can be read simultaneously. Much faster, but historically noisier than CCD because each amplifier is slightly different (causes "fixed-pattern noise"). Your phone camera is CMOS.

sCMOS

Scientific CMOS. A CMOS sensor engineered for science: extremely low read noise (~1 electron), high speed, and large sensor area. The best of both worlds — CCD-like noise with CMOS speed. Modern PIV cameras (pco.edge, Hamamatsu Flash) use sCMOS. However, sCMOS is NOT what you're using — you have an intensified camera.

Your Sensor: CCD + Intensifier

The pco.dicam C1 uses a CCD sensor behind an image intensifier. The intensifier amplifies faint signals before they reach the CCD, giving you single-photon sensitivity that no sCMOS can match. The CCD reads the amplified signal. More on intensifiers in Chapter 3.

Double-Frame Acquisition

PTV requires two images taken in rapid succession (a "frame pair") to track particle movement between frames. The time gap Δt determines the velocity measurement: v = Δx / Δt.

The pco.dicam C1 achieves this with its intensifier — it gates (opens/closes) twice in rapid succession, projecting two particle images onto different halves of the CCD, or onto the same CCD with the intensifier acting as an ultrafast shutter. The minimum interframing time is 300 ns — meaning two images separated by as little as 300 billionths of a second. No mechanical shutter can do this; the intensifier IS the shutter.

Important: double-frame mode doubles the read noise (2.2 e⁻ vs. 1.1 e⁻). For particles near the detection limit, this matters. A particle that's just barely detectable in single-frame mode might be invisible in double-frame mode. This is why VA-2 (single-frame acquisition) is in the test plan — to separate the detection limit from the pairing requirement.

Chapter 3

Image Intensifiers

This is the magic component that makes your camera fundamentally different from every other camera. It amplifies faint light signals by factors of 1,000–100,000× before they reach the sensor.

Why Do You Need an Intensifier?

A 200 nm SiO₂ particle illuminated by a laser pulse might scatter only a few dozen photons toward your camera lens. Of those, maybe 5–10 reach the sensor. A normal camera would record this as noise — indistinguishable from the random dark current fluctuations. An intensifier takes those 5–10 photons and turns them into 5,000–10,000 electrons — a signal that towers above the noise floor.

How It Works: Three Stages

An image intensifier is a tube placed in front of the camera sensor. Light enters one end, gets amplified inside, and exits as a much brighter image on the other end. The three stages are:

Stage 1: The Photocathode

A thin film of semiconductor material coated on the intensifier's front window. When a photon hits it, an electron is ejected (the photoelectric effect). The fraction of photons that successfully produce an electron is the QE (Quantum Efficiency). This is the first and most critical bottleneck.

Photocathode TypeQE at 532 nmDark Count RateBest For
S20 (multialkali)~10%~1,500 /s/cm²UV applications, lowest noise
GaAs (gallium arsenide)~25%~30,000 /s/cm²Good all-rounder, moderate noise
GaAsP (gallium arsenide phosphide)~50%~10,000 /s/cm²Best for 532 nm — 5× more sensitive than S20

GaAsP is the clear winner for your experiment. At 532 nm, GaAsP converts 50% of photons into electrons vs. 10% for S20. That's 5× more signal from the same particle — equivalent to using a 5× more powerful laser. Confirm with the facility that their pco.dicam C1 has a GaAsP photocathode installed.

Stage 2: The MCP (Micro-Channel Plate)

The MCP is a thin glass disk (~0.5 mm thick) with millions of microscopic channels (6 µm diameter in your camera). Each channel is a tiny electron multiplier:

Imagine a bowling alley where each pin, when hit, launches 4 more balls. The first ball knocks over pins, those pins launch more balls, which knock over more pins... One electron enters a channel and bounces down its walls, knocking loose more electrons at each bounce. By the bottom, one electron has become 1,000–10,000. That's the MCP gain.

MCP gain voltage: You control the amplification by adjusting the voltage across the MCP (typically 700–1000 V). Higher voltage = more gain = brighter signal. But also more amplified dark noise. There's a sweet spot for each particle size.

Gain VoltageTypical GainEffect
700 V~100×Low gain — only bright particles visible. Clean images, low noise.
800 V~1,000×Medium gain — good for particles ≥ 500 nm.
900 V~10,000×High gain — dim particles become visible, but noise spots start appearing.
1000 V~100,000×Maximum gain — single photons can be detected, but dark count "ghost particles" are frequent.

Stage 3: The Phosphor Screen

The amplified electron cloud from the MCP hits a phosphor screen, which converts electrons back into light (like an old TV tube). The CCD sensor behind it photographs this phosphor glow.

Phosphor TypeDecay TimeGood For
P43~1 ms (slow)Single-frame imaging. Too slow for double-frame — the first image hasn't faded when the second arrives, causing "ghosting."
P46~0.3 µs (fast)Required for double-frame PTV. Fades 1000× faster than P43, so frame pairs stay separate.

P46 is non-negotiable for your experiment. If the facility has P43, you can only do single-frame imaging (still useful for ηdet, but no PTV pairing). P46 enables both. Confirm before traveling.

Gating: The Ultrafast Shutter

The intensifier can be switched on and off in nanoseconds by controlling the photocathode voltage. This is called gating. A "gate width" of 100 ns means the intensifier is sensitive for only 100 billionths of a second.

Why this matters: during the gate window, signal photons (from the laser-illuminated particles) AND noise photons (from background light, dark counts) accumulate. A shorter gate collects less noise but also less signal. The optimal gate width balances signal collection against noise — this is what VA-6 (gate width sweep) measures experimentally.

The Tandem Lens Coupling

The phosphor screen's glowing image must be relayed to the CCD sensor. In the pco.dicam C1, this is done with a tandem lens with a 0.53:1 scaling. This means the phosphor image (25 mm intensifier diameter) is demagnified to fit the smaller CCD sensor. The consequence: each CCD pixel "sees" a larger area on the intensifier, giving an effective pixel size of ~12.3 µm at the intensifier plane (vs. 6.5 µm actual CCD pixel). This determines your spatial resolution.

Chapter 4

Lenses & Imaging Optics

The lens on the front of the camera determines what you see and how much light you collect. Three numbers define everything: focal length, f-number, and working distance.

Focal Length

The focal length (f) is the distance from the lens center to the sensor when focused at infinity. It determines magnification: a longer focal length gives more zoom (larger magnification) but a narrower field of view.

M = f / (Wd − f) ≈ f / Wd    (for Wd ≫ f)
Magnification ≈ focal length ÷ working distance

For a 50 mm lens at a working distance of 500 mm: M ≈ 50/500 = 0.1. A 100 µm particle would appear as 10 µm on the sensor — about 1.5 pixels with 6.5 µm pixels. You want particle images to span 2–4 pixels for accurate position measurement.

f-Number (The Most Important Lens Setting)

The f-number (f/#) is the ratio of focal length to the aperture (opening) diameter. A smaller f-number means a larger opening → more light collected.

Depth of Field (DOF)

DOF is the range of distances from the lens where objects appear in focus. A wider aperture (smaller f/#) → shallower DOF. For your measurement volume, DOF defines the effective thickness of the "slice" of particles you're imaging — particles outside the DOF appear blurry and get rejected by the detection algorithm.

DOF is like the "sweet spot" of a baseball bat. Swing at f/2.8 and the sweet spot is narrow — only particles in a thin plane get a clean hit. Swing at f/8 and the sweet spot is wide — you see more particles, but each one gives a weaker signal because you collected less light.

Field of View (FOV)

The FOV is the physical size of the scene captured by the camera. It depends on the sensor size and magnification:

FOV = sensor size / M    →    FOV = (2048 × 6.5 µm) / M = 13.3 mm / M
For M = 0.1: FOV ≈ 133 mm. For M = 0.5: FOV ≈ 26.6 mm.

Numerical Aperture (NA) and Solid Angle

The NA quantifies the cone of light the lens captures. For a lens in air: NA ≈ 1/(2 × f/#). The solid angle (fraction of the total sphere of scattered light that enters the lens) scales as NA²:

Ω/4π ≈ π × NA² / (4π) = NA² / 4
At f/2.8: NA = 0.18, Ω/4π = 0.008 → the lens captures ~0.8% of all scattered light

This means 99.2% of the light scattered by the particle goes somewhere the lens can't collect it. This is the fundamental limit — you can increase laser power, but you can never beat the geometry of a finite-aperture lens.

This chain shows a 500 nm particle — ~40 photoelectrons is comfortably detectable. Now consider a 100 nm particle: the scattering cross-section is ~1000× smaller, so only ~0.04 photoelectrons — less than 1. That means most laser pulses produce zero photoelectrons from a 100 nm particle. Detection becomes a probabilistic coin flip.

Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR)

SNR compares the particle signal to the camera noise. Detection requires SNR above a threshold (typically 3–5):

SNR = (Npe × G) / √(Npe × G² + σread² + Ndark × G²)
Npe = photoelectrons, G = MCP gain, σread = read noise, Ndark = dark count electrons

For many photoelectrons (Npe ≫ 1): SNR ≈ √Npe (shot-noise limited — the best case). For very few photoelectrons (Npe ≈ 1): SNR ≈ Npe × G / σread — and increasing MCP gain helps because it lifts the signal above the read noise. This is why the intensifier is essential for small particles: it makes the gain term dominate the read noise.

The Bottom Line

Your camera is a photon-counting machine — not a normal camera. The intensifier converts even a handful of scattered photons from a sub-micron particle into a detectable signal. The limiting factors for your experiment are: ① laser fluence (how many photons hit the particle), ② scattering cross-section (how efficiently the particle scatters those photons — this plummets for d < 300 nm), and ③ the f-number of the lens (how much of the scattered light you capture). The camera itself is not the bottleneck — the physics of scattering is.
